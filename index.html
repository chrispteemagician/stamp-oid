📚 The Philatelist's Library

From penny blacks to modern definitives. Everything Stanley knows — and he knows a lot.

British Stamps — The Big Picture

Penny Black Era (1840-1879)

The world's first postage stamp. Black penny: 1840 only — the plate number matters (1-11). Red pennies followed. Used copies far outnumber mint. Line-engraved printing gives incredible detail. Check for four clear margins and no pen cancellation.

Surface-Printed Issues (1855-1900)

Embossed and surface-printed Victorians. Condition is brutal — used copies common, mint are scarce. The 1867 £5 orange (SG 137) is the holy grail. Watermarks crucial — check every one. Imperforate varieties command huge premiums.

Edwardian & George V (1902-1936)

The 1934 re-engraved definitives. Seahorses (SG 399-401) in high values. George V 1d scarlets — know your shades. The Penny Red plates. Coil stamps with single watermarks.

Modern Definitives (1952-present)

Wilding and Machin definitives. Machin varieties number in the thousands. Phosphor bands — 1, 2, or centre band. Source and date codes. High values from £1 upwards. Cylinder numbers on sheet margins.

World Philately — Key Areas

United States

1847 5c and 10c — the first US stamps. Inverted Jennies. Zeppelin stamps. 1918 24c airmail invert is the most famous US error. Coil stamps and booklet panes. Confederate States — a separate world.

Germany & States

Pre-unification German states are spectacular — Bavaria, Prussia, Thurn & Taxis. Weimar inflation stamps. Third Reich — politically complex, valuable in certain varieties. DDR — underrated.

British Commonwealth

The backbone of most British collections. Canada, Australia, India, South Africa. Commonwealth omnibus issues. High values with impressive designs. Fiscal cancels on high values reduce value significantly.

Classic Rarities

British Guiana 1c magenta (one known). Hawaiian Missionaries. Cape of Good Hope triangulars. Mauritius Post Office stamps. Treskilling Yellow (Sweden). These are the stuff of auction house dreams.

Condition — The Only Thing That Matters

Superb / Gem Very Rare

Perfect centring, full original gum, no faults. Dealers add 200-500% premium. Most "mint" stamps don't qualify.

Very Fine Catalogue Value

Well centred, good margins, fresh colour. This is what catalogue prices assume. Anything below discounts heavily.

Fine 60-80% Cat Value

Slightly off-centre, all margins clear. Most stamps in collections fall here.

Very Good 30-50% Cat Value

Noticeable off-centring, margins touching but clear of design. Hinge remnants on mint.

Good / Poor Filler Only

Perfs into design, thins, tears, stains. Only acceptable as a space-filler until better found.

The Cardinal Sins

  • 🚫 Hinge on a mint never-hinged stamp — ruins the premium
  • 🚫 Regummed stamps — fraud if sold as OG
  • 🚫 Cleaned cancels — tampering, reduces value to nothing
  • 🚫 Thins (back damage) — stamp is permanently compromised
  • 🚫 Trimmed perfs — misrepresented stamps, avoid

❓ Common Questions

The questions Stanley gets asked at every fair. With honest answers.

Are old stamps worth anything?+
Age alone means very little. A stamp's value depends on: rarity (how many were printed), condition (the biggest factor), and demand (what collectors want). A Victorian stamp in poor condition is worth less than a modern commemorative in perfect, never-hinged condition. Condition is everything.
I've inherited a collection — where do I start?+
Don't touch anything until you understand what you have. Don't soak stamps off paper if they're on cover — the whole envelope might be more valuable. Get a Stanley Gibbons catalogue. Photograph everything first. Join a local philatelic society — the expertise is free. Don't rush to sell.
What's a Stanley Gibbons number?+
Stanley Gibbons (SG) is the world's oldest stamp catalogue, published since 1865. Every stamp gets a unique SG number — like a postcode for philately. SG 1 = the 1840 Penny Black. Knowing the SG number lets you look up the current catalogue value and find the exact variety. Scott numbers are the US equivalent.
What are perforations and why do they matter?+
Perforations are the small holes that let you separate stamps. They're measured as the number of holes per 2cm — "perf 14" means 14 holes per 2cm. Different printings can have different perforations, making them different catalogue varieties. Missing perfs (imperforate) between stamps can make an error worth thousands. Count yours with a perforation gauge.
What's a watermark?+
A design embedded in the paper during manufacture, visible when held to light. British stamps have had Crown, Multiple Crown, Multiple St Edward's Crown, and various other watermarks. Same stamp, different watermark = different catalogue number and often very different value. Check with watermark fluid or hold face-down against a light source.
Should I soak stamps off envelopes?+
Depends. Common modern stamps — yes, lukewarm water, fine. But if the envelope has an interesting postmark or is an old first-day cover — keep it on paper. Victorian covers especially — never soak. The whole cover (stamp + envelope + cancellation) can be worth more than the stamp alone. When in doubt, ask Stanley first.
How do I store stamps properly?+
Never use hinges on mint stamps — they damage the gum and destroy the "never hinged" premium. Use stamp mounts (Hawid or Showgard) for mint. Hingeless albums are ideal. Used stamps can be hinged. Store albums away from damp and direct light. Never use rubber bands, sellotape, or paper clips. Cotton gloves for high-value items.
How do I sell my collection?+
Know what you have before selling. Options: auction houses (Spink, Grosvenor, Cavendish — get 2-3 estimates); direct to dealers (quick but expect 40-60% of catalogue value); eBay (good for common material, patience required); philatelic fairs (face-to-face, negotiable). Never sell to a general antiques dealer unless you want a fraction of the value.
